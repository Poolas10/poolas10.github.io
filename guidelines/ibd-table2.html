Consider advanced therapy for mild CD with high-risk features (young, penetrating, extensive). Against requiring step-up failure first (conditional).

No head-to-head preference for naïve patients. Against requiring conventional therapy failure first (conditional).

Biomarkers — Ulcerative Colitis  ·  AGA 2023 (Gastroenterology 164(3):344–372; DOI: 10.1053/j.gastro.2022.12.007)
Clinical Scenario Biomarker Guidance Action / Interpretation Key Cutoffs
Symptomatic Remission — Monitoring
  • Combine biomarker + symptom monitoring rather than symptoms alone (conditional)
  • Monitor every 6–12 months
  • Fecal calprotectin or fecal lactoferrin preferred over CRP alone
  • Normal biomarkers: Rule out active inflammation; avoid routine endoscopy
  • Elevated biomarkers: Endoscopic assessment before treatment adjustment
 FC <150 µg/g, normal fecal lactoferrin, or normal CRP → rules out active inflammation
FC <50 µg/g preferred if recent treatment change (past 1–3 mo) to detect mucosal healing (MES 0 or 1)
Symptomatic Remission — Without Recent Endoscopic Confirmation
  • If no recent endoscopy (within ~3 years) or change in clinical status: consider endoscopic evaluation rather than relying solely on biomarkers
  • Elevated biomarkers in remission without endoscopic confirmation → endoscopy to inform treatment decisions
 FC >150 µg/g + no recent endoscopy → scope before changing therapy
Active Symptoms — Moderate-to-Severe
  • FC or fecal lactoferrin or elevated CRP can rule in active inflammation
  • Avoids routine endoscopy if biomarkers elevated
  • Elevated biomarkers confirm active disease → proceed with treatment adjustment
  • Normal biomarkers with moderate-severe symptoms → endoscopy to rule out alternative diagnosis
 FC >150 µg/g or elevated CRP → active inflammation likely
Biomarker vs. Endoscopy Monitoring Strategy
  • No recommendation for or against biomarker-based monitoring over endoscopy-based monitoring for long-term outcomes (knowledge gap)

Insufficient RCT data comparing strategies head-to-head in UC

 —
All recommendations conditional (weak). Biomarkers covered: serum CRP, fecal calprotectin, fecal lactoferrin. Hospitalized/ASUC not covered by this guideline.
Biomarkers — Crohn's Disease  ·  AGA 2023 (Gastroenterology 165(6):1367–1399; DOI: 10.1053/j.gastro.2023.09.029)
Clinical Scenario Biomarker Guidance Action / Interpretation Key Cutoffs
Asymptomatic / Symptomatic Remission
  • Biomarker + symptom monitoring strategy rather than symptoms alone (conditional)
  • FC and CRP can inform disease management in both asymptomatic and symptomatic disease
  • With recent endoscopic remission confirmed: FC <150 µg/g and/or CRP <5 mg/L rules out active inflammation; avoid routine endoscopy
  • Without recent endoscopic remission: Endoscopic evaluation recommended rather than relying on biomarkers alone
 FC <150 µg/g + CRP <5 mg/L (in stable disease with prior endoscopic remission) → rules out active inflammation
Active Symptoms — Mild
  • Neither normal nor elevated biomarkers alone are sufficiently accurate to determine endoscopic activity in mild symptoms
  • Elevated biomarkers → endoscopic assessment rather than empiric treatment change
  • Normal biomarkers → endoscopic assessment rather than empiric treatment change
 FC >150 µg/g or CRP >5 mg/L with mild symptoms → scope before changing therapy (cannot act on biomarkers alone)
Active Symptoms — Moderate-to-Severe
  • Elevated FC or CRP suggests endoscopic activity → can act without routine endoscopy
  • Biomarker-based assessment + treatment adjustment strategy over symptoms alone (conditional, moderate certainty)
  • Elevated biomarkers → treatment adjustment supported without mandatory endoscopy first
  • Normal biomarkers with moderate-severe symptoms → endoscopy to investigate discordance
 FC >150 µg/g or CRP >5 mg/L with moderate-severe symptoms → active inflammation confirmed; treat accordingly
Post-Op Remission (Surgically Induced) — Low-Risk or on Prophylaxis
  • FC can reliably rule out endoscopic recurrence in low-risk patients on pharmacologic prophylaxis
  • FC <50 µg/g → avoid routine endoscopy (within 12 months post-op)
  • FC <150 µg/g may also rule out recurrence in low-risk + on prophylaxis

CRP alone NOT sufficient to rule out post-op endoscopic recurrence

 FC <50 µg/g (strong threshold); FC <150 µg/g acceptable in low-risk + prophylaxis
Post-Op Remission — High-Risk or Not on Prophylaxis
  • Endoscopic assessment recommended for establishing post-op recurrence in other settings
  • Endoscopy within 6–12 months post-op to assess for recurrence if high-risk or not on prophylaxis
 Do not rely on biomarkers alone — scope required
Endoscopic Healing Index (EHI / Monitr)
  • EHI is a serum protein-based composite biomarker. Studied as surrogate for endoscopic activity in CD.
  • EHI <20: likely endoscopic remission (SES-CD <3)
  • May be used similarly to FC/CRP in clinical decision-making

Less widely adopted than FC/CRP; less data available

 EHI <20 → low likelihood of active endoscopic disease
All recommendations conditional (weak). Biomarkers covered: serum CRP, fecal calprotectin, Endoscopic Healing Index (EHI/Monitr). Fecal lactoferrin not extensively studied in CD.
Pouchitis & Inflammatory Pouch Disorders  ·  AGA 2024 (Gastroenterology 166(1):59–85; DOI: 10.1053/j.gastro.2023.10.015)
Pouch Disorder Type Definition Treatment Recommendations Notes
PRIMARY PREVENTION (pre-pouchitis)
Primary Prevention — Probiotics Post-IPAA, no prior pouchitis episodes
  • No recommendation for or against probiotics for primary prevention (knowledge gap)

Insufficient trial evidence to inform primary prevention with probiotics

 Cost and long-term burden of probiotics must be weighed against limited data on benefit
Primary Prevention — Antibiotics Post-IPAA, no prior pouchitis episodes
  • Against using antibiotics for primary prevention of pouchitis (conditional)
 Risk of antibiotic resistance and adverse effects outweigh unproven preventive benefit
INTERMITTENT POUCHITIS (infrequent episodes, antibiotic-responsive)
Intermittent Pouchitis — Treatment Infrequent episodes; respond to antibiotics; no rapid relapse
  • Antibiotics for treatment — ciprofloxacin or metronidazole for 2–4 weeks (conditional, very low certainty)
  • Combination cipro + metronidazole for refractory acute episodes

Pouchoscopy not required before antibiotic initiation in typical presentations. Scope if atypical symptoms.

 Ciprofloxacin preferred over metronidazole based on tolerability data. Both are first-line options.
CHRONIC ANTIBIOTIC-DEPENDENT POUCHITIS (CADP) — recurrent episodes responsive to antibiotics but relapse off antibiotics
CADP — Prevention of Recurrence Recurrent pouchitis that responds to antibiotics but relapses within days-weeks of stopping
  • Probiotics (multi-strain, e.g. VSL#3) to prevent recurrent pouchitis after antibiotic course (conditional, low certainty)
  • Chronic antibiotic therapy at lowest effective dose with cycling/intermittent gaps (conditional)

Pouchoscopy recommended before escalating beyond antibiotics to confirm diagnosis and rule out Crohn's-like disease

 In patients intolerant to antibiotics or concerned about long-term antibiotic risks: advanced immunosuppressive therapies (biologics/small molecules approved for IBD)
CHRONIC ANTIBIOTIC-REFRACTORY POUCHITIS (CARP) — inadequate response to antibiotics
CARP — Treatment Relapsing-remitting or continuous symptoms that do NOT respond to antibiotics
  • Advanced immunosuppressive therapies — biologics and/or oral small molecules approved for IBD (conditional, low certainty for vedolizumab; very low for others)
  • Corticosteroids (oral budesonide controlled-release preferred) may also be considered

Vedolizumab has best RCT data (EARNEST trial). Adalimumab has retrospective data. Other biologics/JAK inhibitors used by expert consensus.

 Pouchoscopy required before initiating advanced therapy to confirm CARP diagnosis and exclude Crohn's-like disease of the pouch
CROHN'S-LIKE DISEASE OF THE POUCH (CDP) — fistulas, strictures, or prepouch ileitis ≥12 months post-IPAA
Crohn's-like Disease of the Pouch Fistula, stricture, or prepouch ileitis developing ≥12 months after final-stage IPAA surgery
  • Advanced immunosuppressive therapies — biologics and/or oral small molecules approved for IBD (conditional)
  • Corticosteroids may be considered as bridge

Similar treatment approach to CD management. Vedolizumab and anti-TNF agents have supporting data.

 Multidisciplinary evaluation with colorectal surgery recommended. Risk of pouch failure increases with CDP. Consider referral to IBD center of excellence.
CUFFITIS — inflammation of the residual rectal cuff
Cuffitis Inflammation of the residual rectal mucosa (rectal cuff) below the pouch-anal anastomosis
  • Topical 5-ASA suppositories or rectal corticosteroids (first-line, by analogy with UC proctitis management)
  • Advanced immunosuppressive therapy if refractory to topical agents

Guideline recommendations for cuffitis are based primarily on expert opinion; limited RCT data specific to cuffitis

 Distinguish from pouchitis by pouchoscopy — cuffitis involves only the cuff, not the pouch body
When to Scope (Pouchoscopy) —
  • Frequent recurrent episodes (suspected CADP)
  • Inadequate antibiotic response before escalating therapy (suspected CARP)
  • Atypical symptoms
  • When Crohn's-like disease of the pouch is being considered

Not required: Before antibiotics for typical intermittent pouchitis or in infrequent episodes

 Pouchoscopy should include evaluation of the afferent limb (prepouch ileum) and biopsy if Crohn's-like disease is suspected
Source: AGA Clinical Practice Guideline on the Management of Pouchitis and Inflammatory Pouch Disorders. Gastroenterology 2024;166(1):59–85. DOI: 10.1053/j.gastro.2023.10.015. All 9 recommendations are conditional (weak). No ACG pouchitis guideline exists.
